About

I am a cognitive scientist and passionate dancer, interested in how social interaction, embodiment, and neurodiversity shape human–human and human–machine interaction. My research combines experimental psychology with participatory methods to investigate interpersonal coordination.

As a member of the PhD Program International Doctorate for Experimental Approaches to Language And Brain (IDEALAB), I got awarded my PhD in Psychology/Cognitive Science under joint supervision at Macquarie University (Nathan Caruana) and Universität Potsdam (Outi Tuomainen).

Research

My research combines psychology, cognitive science, and linguistics to investigate how autistic and non-autistic people signal, perceive, maintain, and update social information during interaction. I focus on how context shapes individual experience and behaviour, and how action–perception loops can lead to social coordination—or its breakdown.

As a member of the HAVIC Lab at Flinders University, the CHeS Lab at Universität Potsdam, and the Social Brain Sciences Group at ETH Zürich, my PhD research explored how neurodiversity, specifically autism, beliefs about agency, and perceptual cues, such as gaze, shape social interaction in both human–human and human–machine contexts. Using semi-interactive web tasks and immersive VR experiments with eye tracking and motion capture, I investigated how autistic and non-autistic people interpret artificial agents as communicative, and how both agent appearance and perceived control influence these interpretations. My studies demonstrate that autistic participants are not less sensitive to gaze cues but may respond differently depending on agent features, challenging deficit-based assumptions about autism. Building on these findings, I am now conducting studies on attitudes towards AI (in collaboration with Flinders University) and visual perspective taking (in collaboration with UCLouvain).

I am committed to inclusive, participatory, and evidence-based neurodiversity research. In a participatory project, we partnered with autistic people to revise and validate a self-report measure of autistic traits (Comprehensive Autistic Trait Inventory), resulting in the Revised Comprehensive Autistic Trait Inventory (CATI-R). From this work, we also developed guiding principles for creating or revising individual-difference measures, ensuring accessibility, minimising stigma, reducing bias, and capturing diverse experiences without gender-based distortions. Through this collaboration, I learned how research design can either perpetuate exclusion or foster inclusion—depending on whether researchers share power, embed feedback loops, and use language that reflects the diversity of the communities they serve. These insights motivate me to build research cultures that are inclusive, human-centred, and critically aware.

Finally, I strongly endorse open science as a foundation for reproducibility and collaborative research. I preregister studies, share anonymised data and code, and publish in open-access venues.

Teaching Philosophy

As an academic teacher with a background in dance education, I see teaching as a dynamic and reciprocal process that nurtures students' intellectual, emotional, and embodied capacities. My teaching philosophy is grounded in Macquarie University's values of Engage, Empower, and Evolve, with a focus on cultivating reflective, adaptable, and independent learners.

Engage: Learning through Connection and Embodiment

Engagement, for me, begins with presence—being fully attuned to the moment, to students' needs, and to the evolving classroom context. Whether in studio-based practice or academic discussion, I strive to create an environment where students can participate actively and authentically.

  • Active Learning: Classes are student focused, including dialogic teaching and collaborative learning strategies that connect theory to lived experience.
  • Critical Reflection: Reflection is central to my approach: students analyse not only what they learn, but how they learn. Students frequently keep reflection journals to critically engage with content, track their learning process, and relate new insights to their practice or cultural background.
  • Structured Guidance: I provide clear frameworks and supportive scaffolding so students can confidently navigate complex ideas and refine their understanding over time.

Empower: Nurturing Independent and Inclusive Learners

I view education as a practice of empowerment, supporting students to trust their instincts, articulate their questions, and shape their learning paths with confidence and care.

  • Fostering Autonomy: I encourage students to take initiative, engage in peer learning, and take creative risks in selecting their own topics for the final project.
  • Inclusive Practice: I bring a strong commitment to inclusive and culturally responsive pedagogy. I integrate technology to enhance engagement and access (e.g., online polls and asynchronous forums) and offer alternative formats—such as video responses and creative writing—to accommodate diverse learning styles and linguistic backgrounds, particularly in international cohorts.
  • Developing Research and Learning Skills: Beyond content mastery, I aim to equip students with the tools to become self-directed learners and emerging researchers.
  • Open Science and Transparency: In my research, I prioritise open science by preregistering studies, sharing anonymised data and code, and publishing open access. In teaching, I invite students to engage critically with transparency, accessibility, and accountability, and—where possible—to share work in a class repository to foster openness and peer learning.

Evolve: Teaching as a Living, Reflective Practice

Teaching is never static. I view my practice as continuously evolving, shaped by dialogue with colleagues and students, and informed by pedagogical research and reflective inquiry.

  • Professional Growth: I draw on current scholarship in education, embodiment, and the arts to inform my methods and engage regularly in professional development, seeking interdisciplinary conversations that enrich perspectives on teaching, learning, and student wellbeing. Currently, I am completing the one‑year “Teaching Professionals” programme at Universität Potsdam.
  • Feedback and Collaboration: I actively invite feedback from students and treat teaching as a collaborative venture. Understanding what supports or hinders learning helps me adapt courses to individual needs. As in my research practice, I see co‑design and co‑reflection as key to creating meaningful, responsive curricula. Students also practise giving constructive feedback to peers.

Taken together, I strive to build learning environments where students feel seen, challenged, and inspired. I see education as a shared journey—one that honours complexity, cultivates curiosity, and prepares students to move forward with resilience and care.
